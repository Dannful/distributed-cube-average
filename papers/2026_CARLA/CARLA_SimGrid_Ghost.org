* The Paper                                                          :ignore:
** Abstract                                                         :ignore:

#+LaTeX: \begin{abstract}

Ghost cell communication, the asynchronous exchange of halo regions
between neighboring MPI processes, is the canonical technique for
overlapping computation with communication in distributed stencil
applications. Whether this overlap masks communication effectively
depends on the relationship between problem size, partitioning
topology, and interconnection bandwidth, a balance that is difficult
to characterize without access to diverse large-scale hardware. This
paper uses a distributed MPI implementation of the Fletcher RTM
seismic application with 3D block decomposition and asynchronous ghost
cell communication, together with a calibrated SimGrid/SMPI simulation
framework that enables the exploration of network configurations
beyond those physically available.
#+latex: %
Experiments across four host configurations reveal two contrasting
regimes: CPU-only machines naturally achieve near-perfect
communication hiding due to modest floating-point throughput, while
GPU-equipped clusters invert the bottleneck entirely, with accelerator
throughput outpacing the network and stalling compute load near 20% on
1 Gbps interconnects regardless of problem size: a deficit only
recovered at 100 Gbps. Partitioning topology experiments further show
that prime process counts are as detrimental as slow networks, with
slice decompositions falling below single-node throughput baselines
irrespective of bandwidth. Together, these results establish
interconnection bandwidth and partitioning topology as co-determining
factors for ghost cell masking effectiveness, and confirm SimGrid/SMPI
as a practical capacity-planning tool when diverse real infrastructure
is unavailable.

** Introduction

Halo exchange is a foundational technique for distributing iterative
stencil computations across HPC nodes. Each MPI process maintains a
\emph{ghost-cell} layer, a local copy of its neighbors' boundary data,
and asynchronously overlaps these transfers with interior
computation. Whether this overlap achieves genuine communication
masking depends on the interplay of problem size, partitioning
topology, and interconnection bandwidth: with small domains or slow
networks, communication dominates; with large domains and fast
networks, computation can fully hide the transfers
\cite{stencil_supercomputers}. Quantifying this balance requires
systematic exploration of those parameters across hardware
configurations, precisely the kind of exploration that is difficult to
perform on shared, resource-constrained large-scale HPC clusters. The
challenge has motivated dedicated benchmarks such as MiniGhost
\cite{minighost}, a stencil miniapp from the Mantevo suite \cite{heroux2009improving} designed to
explore boundary exchange strategies across problem sizes and process
counts. However, even miniapps require real hardware at scale to
reach observations.

Real HPC infrastructure is expensive, access is time-limited, and it
generally uses fixed hardware configurations. Exploring whether a
100Gbps InfiniBand interconnect would change the masking behavior of
a given application is infeasible when only a 1Gbps Gigabit cluster
is available. The same constraint applies to topology studies:
evaluating a 64-node partition when only five nodes are accessible
requires either analytical modeling, which frequently cannot capture
real memory hierarchy and OS noise, or simulation.

SimGrid/SMPI \cite{simgrid} \cite{simgrid_smpi} addresses this gap by
intercepting MPI communication calls and simulating them on a
configurable virtual platform. At the same time, the application's
computation code executes natively on the host hardware. This strategy
is a calibrated emulation, also known as online simulation, and
therefore not the typical abstract modeling: computation timings are
observed in situ and injected into the simulation, while network
parameters originate from a systematic platform calibration procedure
\cite{simgrid_calibration}. SimGrid's goal is to enable the study of
existing MPI applications within the comfort of the simulator
\cite{simgrid_lower_barriers}. SimGrid's performance prediction
capabilities already exist on communication-intensive benchmarks, most
prominently HPL \cite{hpl_emulation} \cite{hpl_prediction}.

Despite this breadth, published SimGrid use cases tend to focus on
collective operations \cite{degomme2017simulating},
scheduling \cite{dutot2015batsim}, and load
balancing \cite{keller2017using}. Ghost cell and halo exchange studies
exist, but they consistently rely on real hardware or alternative
frameworks. Using SST/macro, one line of work simulates halo exchange
patterns in atmospheric models and notes that SST/macro's packet-level
model and SimGrid produce near-identical communication timing results,
yet the study targets scalability of collective patterns rather than
computation-communication overlap in stencil
kernels \cite{gmd_halo}. SimGrid's SimDag framework has been applied
to evaluate process placement for MPI applications with halo-exchange
communication patterns, focusing on topology-aware optimization of
process affinity rather than masking
effectiveness \cite{simdag_affinity}. SimGrid SMPI has also been used
to predict the performance of stencil computations on heterogeneous
platforms, again centering on placement and mapping strategies rather
than the computation-communication overlap
question \cite{topology_aware}. On real hardware, blocking versus
non-blocking MPI halo exchange has been benchmarked on national
supercomputers, confirming that overlap is achievable but inherently
hardware-specific \cite{blocking_nonblocking_halo}. To the best of our
knowledge, SimGrid/SMPI has not been used to evaluate the
effectiveness of ghost cell masking in MPI stencil applications. Since
SMPI emulates computations between MPI calls on real hardware and
simulates the communication layer with configurable parameters, it is
well-suited for this purpose, and this is the gap the present paper
addresses.

We selected the Fletcher RTM application as the vehicle to make the
evaluation concrete and non-trivial. Fletcher solves pseudo-acoustic
wave equations for seismic imaging in anisotropic media
\cite{fletcher} \cite{fletcher_modeling}, applying an eighth-order
finite-difference stencil over a discretized 3D grid at each time
step. The application is computationally intensive and memory-bound
per iteration, and benefits strongly from GPU acceleration
\cite{multi-gpu} \cite{okamoto2010}. When paired with a slow interconnect,
it creates precisely the bandwidth asymmetry that makes ghost cell
masking most challenging to achieve. Using a real, numerically
validated application rather than a synthetic benchmark ensures that
the communication patterns and memory access behaviors observed are
representative of production workloads.

The contributions of this paper are threefold: *(i)* we briefly present
the design and implementation of a distributed MPI implementation of
Fletcher with 3D block decomposition and asynchronous ghost cell
communication, numerically validated against single-node reference
results; *(ii)* a calibrated SimGrid/SMPI simulation framework for that
application, validated against real cluster executions within a 15%
error margin; and *(iii)* an evaluation that employs SimGrid to
characterize ghost cell masking effectiveness across four host
configurations spanning CPU-only and GPU-equipped clusters, revealing
that CPU-only machines achieve near-perfect communication hiding as a
natural consequence of arithmetic intensity, while GPU-accelerated
clusters demand at least 10--25 Gbps interconnects to escape a
network-bound regime, and that partitioning topology, specifically the
avoidance of prime process counts, is as decisive as bandwidth for
achieving scalable overlap.

** Methods and Materials
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.methods
:END:

This section describes the methods and materials used to build and
evaluate the distributed Fletcher RTM application with ghost cells
using the SimGrid simulation framework. We first establish the
theoretical foundations, static domain partitioning, ghost cell
communication, and the Fletcher pseudo-acoustic stencil, before
detailing the MPI application design that realizes these concepts and
the SimGrid simulation framework used to evaluate performance beyond
the constraints of available hardware.

*** Theoretical Background
**** Partitioning

Static partitioning divides the problem domain once at the start of
execution and keeps the subdivision fixed throughout
\cite{grama2003}. Since the Fletcher stencil kernel applies the same
operation uniformly across the grid, each worker's workload is
homogeneous within its sub-volume, and dynamic rebalancing offers no
benefit. Among the possible 3D decomposition strategies -- Slices
($\{P, 1, 1\}$), Columns ($\{\lfloor P^{1/2}\rfloor, \lfloor P^{1/2}\rfloor, 1\}$), and Blocks ($\{\lfloor
P^{1/3}\rfloor, \ldots\}$) -- the 3D Block decomposition minimizes the total
surface-to-volume ratio of the sub-domains. The total communication
boundary for slices grows as $2(P-1)L^2$, while for blocks it grows as
$6(\sqrt[3]{P}-1)L^2$ \cite{stencil_supercomputers}. Figure
\ref{fig:comm_cost} depicts the communication cost (Y-axis) as a
function of the number of processing elements (PEs, in the X-axis) for
the three strategies (color), where we can see how the 3D approach
scales much better and its advantages appear for small number of
PEs. Because the stencil volume grows as $O(n^3)$ while the halo
surface grows as $O(n^2)$, the communication-to-computation ratio
decreases as problem size grows, provided the decomposition remains
cubic. The ~MPI_Dims_create~ and ~MPI_Cart_create~ functions create a 3D
partitioning that automatically distributes the process count across
the three axes and establishes a Cartesian communicator. A known
pitfall arises with prime process counts: since one cannot use primes
to factor into a balanced cube. We need to pay attention to avoid
using prime numbers for rank counts, as ~MPI_Dims_create~ degenerates to
a $\{P,1,1\}$ slices decomposition, negating the surface-area
advantage of block partitioning.

**** Ghost Cells

Ghost cells extend each process's local grid with a thin shell of
boundary data received from neighboring processes
\cite{ghost_cells}. Although the local stencil does not update these
extra cells, the memory overhead justifies the latency savings. By
prefetching boundary data before it is immediately required, processes
can initiate communication at the start of each iteration and overlap
it with interior computation. The overlap strategy proceeds as follows
within each time step: (1) post non-blocking receives for incoming
halos; (2) compute boundary strips, the cells whose values will be
sent to neighbors, using the previous iteration's complete data; (3)
send boundary strips asynchronously; (4) compute the interior while
halos are in transit; (5) wait for incoming halos, insert them, and
swap wavefield vectors. The critical requirement for effective masking
is that the interior computation time exceeds the halo transfer time;
when the network becomes the bottleneck, step (5) stalls, and
communication dominates the iteration.

**** Fletcher Modeling

Reverse Time Migration (RTM) reconstructs subsurface reflectivity by
propagating seismic wavefields forward and backward in time and
cross-correlating them \cite{rtm}. In isotropic media the wave
equation reduces to $\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} = v^2 \nabla^2 p$; anisotropic
media introduce spatially-varying parameters $\epsilon(x,y,z)$ and $\delta(x,y,z)$
that vary the wave speed with propagation direction
\cite{anisotropy}. Fletcher's pseudo-acoustic formulation
\cite{fletcher} couples the pressure field $p$ with an auxiliary field
$q$ through:

#+BEGIN_EXPORT latex
\begin{gather}
  \frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial t^{2}} = v_{px}^{2}H_{2}p + \alpha v_{px}^{2}H_{1}q + v_{sx}^{2}H_{1}(p - \alpha q) \label{eq:fletcher_p} \\
  \frac{\partial^{2}q}{\partial t^{2}} = \frac{v_{pn}^{2}}{\alpha}H_{2}p + v_{px}^{2}H_{1}q - v_{sx}^{2}H_{2}\!\left(\frac{1}{\alpha}p - q\right) \label{eq:fletcher_q}
\end{gather}
#+END_EXPORT

where $v_{pz}$, $v_{pn}$, $v_{px}$ are derived P-wave velocities, $v_{sx}$ is the
shear velocity, and $H_1$, $H_2$ are differential operators encoding,
respectively, the anisotropic and isotropic components of the stencil
\cite{fletcher_modeling}. The parameter $\alpha$ is set to 1 following
Fletcher's recommendation. The stencil applies this update at every
grid point using an eighth-order finite-difference scheme, advancing
both $p$ and $q$ by one time step $\Delta t$.

*** HPC Application Design with Ghost Cells

The distributed application follows a coordinator-worker model over
MPI. The coordinator, typically in rank 0, partitions the global $N_x
\times N_y \times N_z$ domain using ~MPI_Dims_create~ and dispatches compact
metadata packets, local dimensions, global offsets, absorption zone
parameters, and seismic source position, to all workers via
point-to-point sends. Each worker independently allocates and
initializes its sub-volume, including the four wavefield arrays
($p_{\text{past}}$, $p_{\text{curr}}$, $q_{\text{past}}$, $q_{\text{curr}}$) and
the precomputed anisotropy coefficients; no wavefield data is
distributed, eliminating a potential memory bottleneck on rank 0
during the initialization phase as this application uses synthetic
data only.

The main computation loop implements the five-step ghost cell overlap
strategy described above. Each worker posts non-blocking MPI receives
(~MPI_Irecv~) at the start of each iteration for all 26 neighbors in the
3D Cartesian grid; boundary strips are computed and dispatched via
~MPI_Isend~; interior computation proceeds concurrently; and a final
~MPI_Waitall~ synchronizes all transfers. Because the coordinator
participates in the same Cartesian topology as the workers, it
executes the same computation loop with no special treatment during
the time-stepping phase.  If the ghost-cell strategy for masking
communication is effective, the accumulated time in the ~MPI_Waitall~
operation for any rank should be nearly zero for all ranks.

A CUDA backend replaces the OpenMP propagation kernel when GPU nodes
are available. Wavefield vectors reside permanently in device memory;
only the halo buffers travel between device and host via 3D strided
copies during send/receive operations. The system maps boundary and
interior sub-regions to separate parameterized CUDA kernel launches,
preserving the same overlap logic as the CPU backend. Numerical
validation was performed by comparing SHA-256 checksums (OpenMP
backend, 30 configurations of 1–6 workers across five problem sizes
from $32^3$ to $512^3$) and per-element delta checks within $10^{-15}$
tolerance (CUDA backend), confirming bitwise and near-bitwise
reproducibility against the single-node reference implementation.

*** Simulation with SimGrid

The simulation framework builds on SimGrid/SMPI \cite{simgrid}. We
implement a virtual platform for SimGrid with parameters that adapt it
to different in-premise cluster configurations. The main parameters
are the host speed (in GFlops) and the network bandwidth (in
Gbps). Since we run all simulations on a single node per cluster and
the compute code runs either with OpenMP or CUDA, the speed parameter
for all hosts in the virtual platform matches that of the host running
the simulation. Rather than replacing compute kernels with analytical
FLOP counts, which proved an oversimplification that ignored cache
behavior, PCIe transfers, and memory copies, we leverage SimGrid's
native computation sampling: the main propagation kernel executes in
full for a variable number of warm-up iterations, after which the code
injects stable timing samples into the simulation. We vary the network
bandwidth parameter to reflect our intentions when evaluating masking
effectiveness. Since the virtual platform accepts customized values
after a calibration procedure, we follow a methodology
\cite{simgrid_calibration} to extract MPI runtime parameters, protocol
thresholds, bandwidth, and latency from a systematic suite of
microbenchmarks. We adopt these calibrated values throughout the
simulations. Our post-calibration validation shows that the platform
description reproduces the real cluster's behavior within a 15% error
margin for message sizes up to $\approx$1,GB, which we consider acceptable
since application behavior, both compute and communication, remains
very homogeneous throughout execution, indicating that trends stay
sufficiently faithful to evaluate communication masking effectiveness.

*** Evaluation Methodology and Masking Effectiveness Metric

We define two Design of Experiment (DoE) depending whether the host
machines has a GPU or is solely based on CPU cores. If the host
machine used for simulation contains a GPU, the GPU DoE consists of a
full factorial structure, crossing three independent factors to
produce 120 unique experimental conditions. The first factor, *(1)
problem size*, varies across four levels (64, 128, 256, and 512), where
each number represents the order of the cubic problem domain. The
second factor, *(2) number of hosts*, spans five levels (4, 8, 16, 27,
and 64), covering a small cluster (4 nodes) up to a larger deployment
(64 nodes). The choice for these levels is aligned with the 3D
partitioning. The third factor, *(3) network bandwidth*, takes six
levels (1, 2.5, 10, 25, 50, and 100 Gbps), spanning three orders of
magnitude from low-speed to high-speed interconnects.  If the host
machine used for simulation contains only CPU cores, the CPU DoE is
different because of the smaller compute power and consists also of a
full factorial structure, crossing the same three factors to produce
128 unique experimental conditions. The problem size factor contains
four levels (64, 128, 256, and 512). The number of hosts factor spans
four levels (8, 16, 32, and 64). Finally, the network bandwidth factor
takes eight levels (10, 25, 40, and 50 Mbps, followed by 1, 2.5, 10,
and 100 Gbps), covering an exceptionally wide range spanning four
orders of magnitude, from slow interconnects typical of commodity or
congested networks up to high-speed links found in modern HPC
environments. In both DoE projects, the application executes 1000
iterations, the network latency remains fixed at 22\micro seconds (taken
from our real network measurements), and each of the combinations
appears exactly once, with no replication, and is assigned a
sequential run order.  The design aims to jointly characterize how
performance scales with workload size, platform scale, and network
capacity in a distributed computing or HPC context, with particular
emphasis on understanding the impact of network bandwidth across a
broad and heterogeneous range of speeds with high compute capacity
(large number of GPUs) and CPU-only clusters.

We consider these hosts to run the simulations with their respective
compute configurations: =tupi= (GPU RTX4090), =poti= (GPU RTX4070), =cei=
(CPU 2\times Xeon Silver 4116), and =tsubasa= (2\times Xeon Gold 6226). When
running the simulations in GPU-equipped, we run the optimized CUDA
code. When in CPU-equipped, we run the OpenMP kernel that exploits all
CPU cores (both logical and physical). We carry out all simulation
using calibrated values for MPI communications based on the real =poti=
cluster network and per-DoE network bandwidth configurations.

The *Masking Effectiveness* metric derives from MPI communication
traces. Figure \ref{fig:masking-example} depicts two examples of
simulation runs to depict the metric rationale. SimGrid generates
these traces with built-in features that can easily be used. We first
isolate the core execution window, defined as the interval between the
earliest =MPI_Irecv= start and the last =MPI_Waitall= end across all
ranks, and then clipping all events to that window. A Compute state is
synthetically inferred for each container by identifying gaps between
consecutive MPI communication events. For each rank, the total
communication duration and the overall makespan (from the first to the
last event) are summed across all ranks, and the Communication ratio
is obtained by dividing the aggregate communication duration by the
aggregate makespan. Finally, Masking Effectiveness is defined as one
minus that Communication ratio, representing the fraction of the
execution time during which communication was successfully overlapped
with or hidden by computation: the closer to one, the more effectively
the application masks its communication overhead. We employ this
metric in our results interpretation as it clearly reflects how much
communicating hiding a particular configuration (problem size, number
of workers, and network capacity) delivers as behavior. Figure
\ref{fig:masking-example} shows exactly this perception: in the left,
a very communication-bound run with masking of only 0.0819; in the
right, a very good example where masking is of 0.792.

From a practical perspective, all MPI traces obtained from
SimGrid/SMPI pass through modern data science tools based on the R
ecosystem, primarily leveraging the tidyverse and ggplot2 stack. Raw
trace files generated during execution are first converted into
structured tabular representations, enabling scalable manipulation of
the events and the computation of our metric.

*** CPU Cluster: Communication Masking Progression

Figure \ref{fig:cei-masking} illustrates the results for the =cei=
machine (a CPU-only host equipped with dual Xeon Silver 4116
processors) revealing a striking pattern: communication masking is
nearly trivially achieved whenever the network bandwidth is
sufficiently low relative to the available compute throughput. Across
all problem sizes (64³ to 512³) and host counts (8 to 64), Masking
Effectiveness remains below 0.30 for bandwidths at or below 100 Mbps,
reflecting configurations in which communication dominates and there
is little computation to hide it behind. However, as soon as bandwidth
crosses the 1 Gbps threshold, the picture changes dramatically: for
the largest problem size (512³) with only 8 hosts, Masking
Effectiveness leaps to approximately 0.94, and even moderately sized
problems (256³, 8 hosts) reach values above 0.89 at 1 Gbps. This sharp
transition reveals that the CPU-only cei cluster sits in a
compute-bound regime for most realistic HPC network configurations:
once the network is fast enough to deliver data quickly, the
relatively modest per-core floating-point throughput of the Xeon
Silver 4116 ensures that computation fills the gaps between
communication events almost entirely, yielding near-perfect
masking. Put differently, on a slow CPU-only cluster, communication
masking is essentially free: the application's arithmetic intensity
naturally outpaces the network, so overlap emerges naturally, making
this class of hardware surprisingly forgiving from an MPI
communication-hiding perspective.

The =tsubasa= machine (a CPU-only host with dual Xeon Gold 6226
processors, representing a somewhat more powerful CPU platform than
=cei=) fully corroborates the same trend. We observe that Masking
Effectiveness stays near zero for sub-100 Mbps bandwidths across all
problem sizes and host counts, but surges past 0.90 at 1 Gbps for the
largest problem size (512³) with 8 hosts, and remains consistently
high (above 0.80) for large problems at faster interconnects. The
slightly higher core count and clock speed of the Gold 6226 relative
to the Silver 4116 do not fundamentally alter the picture: the
compute-bound nature of the application on CPU-only hardware ensures
that, as soon as the network delivers data at a reasonable pace,
computation effortlessly absorbs the communication latency.

Both =cei= and =tsubasa= have a coherent story: on CPU-only clusters,
near-perfect communication masking (closer to one in our plots) is
easily achievable as a consequence of arithmetic intensity exceeding
network throughput, requiring no special software
tuning. Interestingly, the =cei= cluster has a 10 Gbps network so one
might already expect good ghost cell capability for communication
hiding in this cluster.

*** GPU Cluster: The Network Bottleneck

Figure \ref{fig:poti-masking} illustrates the results for the =poti= GPU
cluster, revealing the opposite regime. With RTX4070 accelerators
paired with a 1Gbps Gigabit network (first X-axis coordinate), the
masking effectiveness stalls at a very low regime, at around 0.25,
regardless of problem size: $128^3$, $256^3$, and $512^3$ all yield
the same result. Even the largest problem (512³) with only 4 hosts
barely reaches 0.16, and the metric deteriorates further with more
hosts, confirming that the RTX4070 already outpaces the Gigabit link
regardless of domain size: the RTX4070 finishes the local stencil
computation so rapidly that the Gigabit network cannot deliver ghost
cells in time.  Each of the 1K iterations is therefore dominated by
=MPI_Waitall= stalls. This behavior is precisely the saturation
described in the theoretical background: the volume/boundary ratio
offers no relief because the GPU's throughput overwhelms any benefit
from larger domains. Our simulations indicate that the recovery begins
at 10 Gbps, where 512³ with 4 hosts climbs to 0.97 and stays there
through 100 Gbps. However, smaller problems and larger host counts
demand even higher bandwidth to recover meaningful overlap due to
their less favorable communication-to-computation ratio. The poti data
thus reveal the observation that to escape the network-bottleneck
regime on our =poti= GPU cluster requires at minimum a 10 Gbps or 25
Gbps interconnect, and even then only under favorable problem-size and
node-count conditions.

network-bottleneck regime observed on the =poti= cluster: at 1 Gbps,
Masking Effectiveness collapses across all configurations, with even
the largest problems barely exceeding 0.16 for 4 hosts and falling
below 0.08 for 27 or more, reflecting an accelerator so fast that the
Gigabit link is perpetually the gating factor. Meaningful overlap
resurfaces only at 10 Gbps and above, where small host counts and
large domains recover values above 0.96. These are consistent with the
poti pattern, only shifted toward demanding even higher bandwidth due
to the RTX4090's greater raw throughput. However, tupi exposes a
striking anomaly absent from poti: Masking Effectiveness keeps
improving from 256³ to 512³ only with larger host counts (27 and 64)
for network speed 25 Gbps and larger. Lower hosts counts (4, 8,
and 16) have a poorer metric in 512³.  This reversal of the expected
scaling behavior is likely governed by the same volume-to-surface
ratio dynamics that drive communication efficiency in domain
decomposition: as the problem size grows from 256³ to 512³, the
interior compute volume scales as the cube of the domain edge, while
the ghost-cell communication surface grows only as its square. With
few hosts, each rank owns a large subdomain and already benefits from
a favorable ratio, so doubling the edge length adds proportionally
more communication surface than it does useful compute to overlap,
degrading masking. With many hosts, each rank's subdomain is small and
surface-dominated at 256³, and the jump to 512³ finally brings enough
interior work to amortize the communication cost, pushing Masking
Effectiveness upward.

*** Masking Effectiveness Heatmap: Find Optimal Configurations

Figure \ref{fig:gpu-masking-heatmap} presents a joint visualization of
the Masking Effectiveness across all experimental conditions for the
two GPU-equipped hosts, =poti= and =tupi=, as a function of network
bandwidth (X-axis), number of hosts (Y-axis), and problem size
(facets). The heatmap format allows one to immediately pinpoint, for
any given configuration explored in our DoE, whether communication is
effectively hidden by computation or whether the application stalls
waiting for ghost cells. The viridis color scale, from dark purple
(poor masking) to bright yellow (near-perfect masking), reveals a
consistent transition pattern: the lower-left region of each panel,
corresponding to low bandwidth and many hosts, is uniformly dark,
confirming network-bound stalls regardless of problem
size. Conversely, the lower-right region, large domains, few hosts,
and high bandwidth, concentrates the bright cells where masking
effectiveness exceeds 0.90.  The precise identification of this
transition boundary is one of the key contributions of the
SimGrid/SMPI simulation approach: rather than requiring exhaustive
real-cluster experiments across dozens of network configurations, the
calibrated simulator allows us to navigate the full factorial space
and pinpoint exactly which (size, hosts, bandwidth) triplets deliver
effective communication hiding, information that would be
prohibitively expensive to obtain on real infrastructure alone.

** Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.conclusion
:END:

This paper presented a distributed MPI implementation of the Fletcher
RTM application with 3D block decomposition and asynchronous ghost
cell communication, alongside a calibrated SimGrid/SMPI simulation
framework for exploring network configurations beyond those physically
available. Experiments across four host configurations (cei, tsubasa,
poti, and tupi) spanning both CPU-only and GPU-equipped clusters
exposed two sharply contrasting regimes. On CPU-only machines, modest
floating-point throughput naturally yields near-perfect communication
hiding across most network conditions: the cei cluster reaches
approximately 87% compute load at 512³ on its real 10 Gbps network,
and tsubasa corroborates the same trend, establishing that arithmetic
intensity alone is sufficient to mask communication on this class of
hardware without any special tuning. On GPU-equipped clusters, the
picture inverts entirely: the raw throughput of RTX4070 and RTX4090
accelerators makes network bandwidth the governing bottleneck, with
both poti and tupi stalling near 20% compute load on a 1 Gbps
interconnect regardless of problem size.

Our design of experiments, sweeping network bandwidth across six levels
from 1 Gbps to 100 Gbps, demonstrated a clear and systematic
progression toward computation-bound behavior, with Masking
Effectiveness values exceeding 90% achieved at 100 Gbps on the GPU
clusters. The joint heatmap of Masking Effectiveness across all
(problem size, host count, bandwidth) triplets provides a single,
immediately readable map of the transition boundary between
network-bound and compute-bound regimes, a kind of capacity-planning
chart for practitioners deciding whether a network upgrade is
worthwhile for a given workload. This is precisely where
SimGrid/SMPI's value is most visible: by emulating computations
between MPI calls on real hardware while simulating the communication
layer with freely configurable parameters, it navigates the full
factorial experimental space at a fraction of the cost of real-cluster
deployments. To the best of our knowledge, SimGrid/SMPI has not
previously been used to evaluate the effectiveness of ghost cell
masking in MPI stencil applications; the present work demonstrates
that it is not only applicable but highly informative for this
purpose, and that calibrated simulation is a practical and credible
alternative to exhaustive real-hardware experimentation.

Future work includes experiments on the Santos Dumont supercomputer to
