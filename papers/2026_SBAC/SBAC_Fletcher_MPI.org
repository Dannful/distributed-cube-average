** Abstract                                                         :ignore:

#+LaTeX: \begin{abstract}

Distributing iterative stencil computations across HPC nodes requires
careful design of inter-process communication, particularly to overlap
halo exchanges with interior computation using ghost cells. Whether
such overlap achieves genuine communication masking depends on the
relationship between problem size, partitioning topology, and network
bandwidth. This paper presents a distributed MPI implementation of the
Fletcher RTM seismic application using 3D block decomposition and
asynchronous ghost cell communication, with GPU acceleration via
CUDA. Experiments on real HPC clusters reveal two sharply contrasting
regimes: CPU-only machines achieve near-perfect communication hiding
(75--87% compute load) due to modest floating-point throughput, while
GPU-equipped clusters invert the bottleneck entirely: the RTX4070
accelerator outpaces a 1Gbps interconnect, and stalls compute load
near 20% regardless of problem size. Calibrated SimGrid SMPI
simulations demonstrate a smooth, bandwidth-driven transition from
communication-bound to computation-bound behavior, with GPU compute
load rising from \approx20% at 1Gbps to \approx75--80% at 10Gbps and \approx85--90% at
100Gbps. Partitioning topology is shown to be as decisive as
bandwidth: prime process counts force slice decompositions that yield
negative speedup relative to the single-node baseline, with a 5-worker
configuration falling below the 3870 MSamples/s baseline while an
8-worker configuration reaches 8309 MSamples/s (10Gbps) and 9374
MSamples/s (100Gbps). Together, these results establish that
interconnection bandwidth and the partitioning topology are
co-determining factors for ghost cell masking effectiveness in
distributed stencil applications.

#+LaTeX: \end{abstract}

** Introduction
*** Context                                                        :ignore:

Halo exchange is a foundational technique for distributing iterative
stencil computations across HPC nodes. Each MPI process maintains a
/ghost-cell/ layer, which is a local copy of its neighbors' boundary
data, and asynchronously overlaps these transfers with interior
computation. Whether this overlap achieves genuine communication
masking depends on the interplay of problem size, partitioning
topology, and interconnection bandwidth: with small domains or slow
networks, communication dominates; with large domains and fast
networks, computation can fully hide the transfers
\cite{stencil_supercomputers}. When correctly tailored to the variety
of configurations, the benefits of halo exchange are undeniable.

The Fletcher method solves pseudo-acoustic wave equations for seismic
imaging in anisotropic media \cite{fletcher, fletcher_modeling},
applying an eighth-order finite-difference stencil over a discretized
3D grid at each time step.  The application is computationally
intensive and memory-bound per iteration, and benefits strongly from
GPU acceleration \cite{multi-gpu, okamoto2010}.

Early contributions targeted GPU memory efficiency on single-node
systems. Pavan et al. applied shared memory, read-only cache, and
register reuse strategies to Fletcher-based stencil kernels, achieving
performance improvements of up to 264.6% on NVIDIA P100
\cite{pavan2018wperf}. I/O was identified as a complementary
bottleneck, and checkpointing strategies reduced RTM execution time by
up to 17.33% \cite{pavan2018wscad}. A significant algorithmic advance
was the reformulation of the Fletcher cross-derivative terms, reducing
floating-point operations and yielding up to 73.6% performance and
42.6% energy efficiency improvements \cite{serpa2020erad}; these
optimizations were subsequently integrated into a systematic
comparison across multicore and GPU architectures using OpenMP,
OpenACC, and CUDA, establishing that CUDA on NVIDIA Volta delivers
peak performance. In comparison, OpenACC provides a portable
alternative with less than 10--20% degradation \cite{serpa2019wscad}
\cite{serpa2021ccpe}. At the multi-GPU level, Rigon et al. overlapped
inter-device border-region communication with computation, reducing
the energy-delay product by up to 40% \cite{rigon2024erad}. In
comparison, Schussler et al. proposed a genetic-algorithm-driven
autotuner (BTO) for CUDA thread-block configurations that achieves up
to 83.8% EDP improvement over default execution on both AMD and NVIDIA
GPUs \cite{schussler2024pdp}. Portability to novel architectures has
also been investigated. Künas et al. evaluated SYCL with ESIMD and
subgroup optimizations for the Fletcher/RTM kernel on Intel Max GPU,
obtaining speedups of up to 4.6\times \cite{kunas2025carla}, and Lorenzon
et al. assessed twelve programming-model implementations across eleven
GPUs from NVIDIA and AMD, reporting that HIP and RAJA achieve the
highest carbon-efficiency portability \cite{lorenzon2025sbacpad}. At
the systems level, Machado et al. deployed the Fletcher application on
cloud SaaS instances \cite{machado2023sbacpadw}, and introduced
MPI-based strategies to overlap computation with disk I/O, achieving
speedups of up to 1.85\times \cite{machado2026ccpe}.

Several related efforts address halo exchange and stencil
communication from complementary angles. Pearson et
al. \cite{stencil_supercomputers} show that 3D block decomposition
minimizes the total communication boundary for Cartesian stencil
applications on heterogeneous supercomputers, and develop a node-aware
MPI+CUDA assignment strategy that maps subdomains to GPUs based on
inter-GPU communication bandwidth. Okamoto et al. \cite{okamoto2010}
accelerate seismic wave propagation on multi-GPU clusters with domain
decomposition and ghost-point exchanges, observing that communication
overhead scales as $N^{2/3}$ with the number of GPUs and that small
domains suffer severely from network latency, motivating the need for
large problem sizes. Van den Berg et al. \cite{gmd_halo} study MPI
halo exchange patterns for atmospheric models using SST/macro and
SimGrid, but with a focus on the scalability of collective patterns
rather than computation-communication overlap in stencil
kernels. Mercier and Jeannot \cite{simdag_affinity} evaluate process
placement for MPI applications with halo-exchange communication,
centering on topology-aware optimization of process affinity. Wittmann
et al. \cite{blocking_nonblocking_halo} benchmark blocking versus
non-blocking halo exchange for Lattice Boltzmann methods on real
supercomputers, confirming that overlap is achievable but inherently
hardware-specific. In the context of seismic applications, as
previously explicited, Rigon et al. \cite{multi-gpu} distribute the
Fletcher load across multiple GPUs within a single node using
device-to-device ghost-cell transfers, achieving a speedup of 2.77 for
large domains but observing severe degradation on small grids due to
synchronization overhead.

Despite this extensive body of work, all existing multi-GPU Fletcher
implementations \cite{multi-gpu, okamoto2010} are limited to a single
computational node, operating either within a single compute node or
within a multi-GPU server that relies on shared memory or peer-to-peer
GPU links. This single-node limitation imposes immediate scalability
constraints in both computational throughput and GPU memory. The
natural path to resolving this bottleneck is to distribute computation
across multiple nodes via proper domain partitioning and inter-node
asynchronous communication. As far as we know, the distribution of the
Fletcher method implementation across multiple cluster nodes via MPI,
with ghost-cell halo exchange to manage subdomain boundary data, has
not been explored. There is a clear gap in the literature, leaving
open the question of how partitioning topology and network bandwidth
jointly determine communication-overlap effectiveness at large-scale
cluster deployments for this application. Although implementing the
halo exchange remains relatively easy, the challenge lies in
leveraging it in realistic scenarios. For example, when paired with a
slow network, it creates precisely the bandwidth asymmetry that
makes the method most challenging to achieve. GPUs 
finish local stencil computation far faster than the network can
deliver boundary data. It is therefore necessary not only to design
the halo exchange but also to conduct a thorough performance analysis
to address these challenges.

The contributions of this paper are: *(i)* a distributed MPI
implementation of the Fletcher application with 3D block decomposition
and asynchronous ghost cell communication, numerically validated
against the single-node reference implementation across several
configurations; *(ii)* GPU acceleration via CUDA with wavefield data
permanently resident in device memory, preserving the same
computation-communication overlap strategy as the CPU backend; and
*(iii)* an evaluation on real HPC clusters showing how network bandwidth
and partitioning topology jointly determine ghost cell masking
effectiveness, with compute loads ranging from approximately 20% on a
1Gbps GPU cluster to above 87% on a 10Gbps CPU cluster. *(iv)* an
evaluation through emulation to anticipate the necessary network
capacity to perfectly leverage the halo exchange for hiding
communication latency.  These contributions collectively establish
that ghost cell effectiveness is inherently application- and
platform-dependent, and scales with problem size, partitioning shape,
and available bandwidth.

** Methods and Materials
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.methods
:END:

This section describes the methods and materials used to build and
evaluate the distributed Fletcher RTM application with ghost cells. We
first establish the theoretical foundations (the Fletcher
pseudo-acoustic stencil, static domain partitioning, and ghost cell
communication) before detailing the MPI application design that
realizes these concepts and the evaluation methodology used to
characterize performance on HPC clusters.

*** Theoretical Background
**** Fletcher Modeling

RTM recovers subsurface reflectivity by cross-correlating forward- and
backward-propagated seismic wavefields \cite{rtm}. In isotropic media the
governing equation is simply $\frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} = v^2 \nabla^2
p$; anisotropic media require spatially-varying parameters $\epsilon(x,y,z)$
and $\delta(x,y,z)$ that make wave speed direction-dependent \cite{anisotropy}.
Fletcher's pseudo-acoustic formulation \cite{fletcher} couples the pressure
field $p$ with an auxiliary field $q$ through:

#+BEGIN_EXPORT latex
\begin{gather}
  \frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial t^{2}} = v_{px}^{2}H_{2}p + \alpha v_{px}^{2}H_{1}q + v_{sx}^{2}H_{1}(p - \alpha q) \label{eq:fletcher_p} \\
  \frac{\partial^{2}q}{\partial t^{2}} = \frac{v_{pn}^{2}}{\alpha}H_{2}p + v_{px}^{2}H_{1}q - v_{sx}^{2}H_{2}\!\left(\frac{1}{\alpha}p - q\right) \label{eq:fletcher_q}
\end{gather}
#+END_EXPORT

where $v_{pz}$, $v_{pn}$, $v_{px}$ are derived P-wave velocities, $v_{sx}$ is the
shear velocity, and $H_1$, $H_2$ are differential operators encoding
the anisotropic and isotropic stencil components, respectively
\cite{fletcher_modeling}. The parameter $\alpha$ is set to 1 following
Fletcher's recommendation. At every grid point, an eighth-order
finite-difference scheme advances both $p$ and $q$ by a time step $\Delta
t$.

**** Partitioning
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.partitioning
:END:

Because the Fletcher stencil applies identical operations uniformly
across the grid, each processing element inherits a balanced workload,
making dynamic load redistribution unnecessary. Consequently, a static
domain decomposition, where the spatial division is determined once at
initialization and remains unchanged throughout execution, is
sufficient \cite{grama2003}. When evaluating three-dimensional grid
partitions, three primary configurations are typically considered:
slices ($\{P, 1, 1\}$), columns ($\{\lfloor P^{1/2}\rfloor, \lfloor P^{1/2}\rfloor, 1\}$), and
blocks ($\{\lfloor P^{1/3}\rfloor, \ldots\}$). The block layout is preferred because
it minimizes the surface-to-volume ratio of each subdomain. This
geometric advantage directly reduces communication overhead:
slice-based partitions incur a halo boundary scaling as $2(P-1)L^2$,
whereas block partitions scale more favorably as $6(\sqrt[3]{P}-1)L^2$
\cite{stencil_supercomputers}. Since the computational workload within
a stencil volume grows as $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ while the halo exchange
area scales as $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$, the communication-to-computation
ratio naturally decreases as problem size increases, provided the
decomposition remains approximately cubic.  Figure \ref{fig:comm_cost}
confirms this analytically: as $P$ grows, the internal boundary area
of the Slices strategy rises steeply and nearly linearly, the Columns
strategy grows at an intermediate rate proportional to $\sqrt{P}$, and
the Blocks strategy remains the flattest curve, growing only as
$\sqrt[3]{P}$. The divergence between strategies widens with scale, so
the relative benefit of 3D Block decomposition becomes increasingly
pronounced at large process counts.  In practice, this 3D partition is
established using \texttt{MPI\_Dims\_create} and
\texttt{MPI\_Cart\_create}, which automatically compute balanced axis
lengths and initialize a Cartesian communicator. A significant caveat
arises when $P$ is prime: because primes lack nontrivial factors,
\texttt{MPI\_Dims\_create} defaults to a $\{P, 1, 1\}$ slice layout,
effectively negating the communication efficiency gains of a true
block decomposition.

**** Ghost Cells
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.ghost_cells
:END:

Each process augments its local grid with a thin shell of ghost cells
holding boundary data exchanged with neighbouring processes
\cite{ghost_cells}. These extra cells are never updated by the
stencil, but the memory cost is offset by the ability to prefetch halo
data ahead of when it is needed. Within each time step, communication
and computation are overlapped as follows: (1) post non-blocking
receives (\texttt{MPI\_Irecv}) for incoming halos; (2) compute the
boundary strips to be sent, using the previous iteration's complete
data; (3) dispatch them asynchronously (\texttt{MPI\_Isend}); (4)
compute the interior while halos are in transit; (5) wait for all
incoming halos (\texttt{MPI\_Waitall}), insert them, and swap
wavefield vectors. This scheme hides communication latency only when
interior computation takes longer than the halo transfer; if the
network is the bottleneck, step (5) blocks and communication overhead
becomes the dominant cost.

*** Distributed Fletcher RTM Design

Our implementation of the MPI-based 3D Fletcher uses a
coordinator-worker model. The coordinator (rank 0) partitions the
global $N_x \times N_y \times N_z$ domain using \texttt{MPI\_Dims\_create} and
dispatches compact metadata packets (local dimensions, global offsets,
absorption zone parameters, and seismic source position) to all
workers via point-to-point sends. Crucially, the coordinator does not
distribute any wavefield data. Each worker independently allocates and
initializes its own sub-volume, including the four wavefield arrays
($p_{\text{past}}$, $p_{\text{curr}}$, $q_{\text{past}}$, $q_{\text{curr}}$) and
the precomputed anisotropy coefficients. This design eliminates a
central memory bottleneck at rank zero, a concern well documented in the
literature for large-scale seismic workloads, where per-node memory,
particularly GPU memory, is the primary scaling constraint. The same
principle extends to the output phase. Rather than funneling all
wavefield data back through the coordinator for a single write,
results are collected in a streaming fashion, avoiding a second
centralization bottleneck at finalization. The code accounts for an
expanded global domain to accommodate absorption zones at the
boundaries and the additional cells required by the eighth-order
finite-difference stencil, mirroring the behavior of the reference
public implementation of the Fletcher method.

The main computation loop executes a fixed number of time steps that
advance the wavefield simulation governed by Fletcher's equations in
anisotropic media using a five-step ghost cell overlap strategy. At
the start of every iteration, a seismic source is injected into the
grid cell owned by whichever process contains the source location. The
loop then proceeds through five phases: first, ~MPI_Irecv~ calls are
posted for the ghost-cell data expected from all 26 neighbors on the
3D Cartesian topology, covering both wavefield components =p= and =q=;
second, the boundary strips of each local partition, the outermost
cells whose values will be sent to neighbors, are propagated forward
using a stencil-based finite-difference kernel (CUDA-accelerated or
OpenMP-parallelized depending on the build), and the updated halo
faces are immediately dispatched asynchronously via ~MPI_Isend~ to each
neighbor; third, while these transfers are in flight, the interior
region, stripped of its halo layers, is advanced concurrently; fourth,
a final ~MPI_Waitall~ synchronizes all pending communication requests,
and the incoming halo slices are inserted into the corresponding ghost
regions; fifth, the wavefield buffers are swapped to prepare for the
next iteration. Because the coordinator rank participates in the same
Cartesian topology as the workers, all processes execute the identical
computation loop with no special treatment during time-stepping. When
the overlap strategy is effective, the time spent in ~MPI_Waitall~
remains negligible across all ranks, since interior computation fully
hides communication latency. Once all iterations are complete, each
non-coordinator process sends its final $p_{curr}$ and $q_{curr}$ fields
back to the coordinator via ~MPI_Send~, which gathers and writes to
output files.

At the heart of each time step, every thread (CUDA block thread or
OpenMP parallel iteration) computes a nine-point stencil update for
two coupled wavefield variables, p and q, using a leapfrog
finite-difference scheme. For each grid point, second-order
derivatives are evaluated in all three principal directions (xx, yy,
zz) as well as three mixed directions (xy, yz, xz), producing six
second-derivative terms per variable. These are then combined through
a coordinate transformation parameterized by anisotropy angles (theta
and phi) to form two auxiliary operators, which capture the
directional dependence of wave propagation in
tilted-transverse-isotropic (TTI) media. The right-hand sides of the
Fletcher equations are assembled as weighted sums, where the weights
are the squared P- and S-wave velocities derived from the local
anisotropic velocity model. Finally, the new-time-step values are
written via explicit leapfrog recurrence, advancing the ~p~ and ~q~ fields
simultaneously while reusing the previous two time levels.  When
multiple MPI processes share a node with multiple GPUs, each process
is assigned a GPU based on its rank, distributing processes cyclically
across all available devices without manual configuration.

Numerical validation is performed externally by comparing binary
output files from the single-node reference run against our
implementation with both OpenMP and CUDA backends. Each output file
contains both wavefield components packed as a contiguous array of
single-precision floats. For the OpenMP backend, reproducibility is
assessed by computing =SHA-256= checksums across 30 configurations
spanning 1--6 workers and five problem sizes from $32^3$ to $512^3$,
confirming bitwise-identical output. For the CUDA backend, per-element
delta checks are performed with a tolerance of $10^{-15}$, confirming
near-bitwise agreement that reflects the expected accumulation of
floating-point rounding differences between CPU and GPU execution
paths. We validate therefore that the numerical results of our
implementation against reference single-node implementations.

*** Evaluation Methodology

We run experiments on two clusters of the PCAD platform at INF/UFRGS:
one CPU-only and one GPU-equipped, targeting distinct
compute-to-communication regimes.

**** CPU Cluster

We conduct CPU experiments on the /cei/ cluster of the PCAD platform at
INF/UFRGS. The cluster consists of six Supermicro X11DPU systems,
functioning as computational nodes, connected by a 10Gbit
network. Each node is equipped with 2\times Intel Xeon Silver 4116
processors at 2.1 GHz, offering 48 threads with 24 cores and 96GB DDR4
RAM. Experiments use OpenMP to leverage each node's parallelism. We
trace every execution with akypuera \cite{akypuera}, providing low
observation overhead to record every MPI operation performed by each
process. Traces are converted to CSV using the PajeNG tool
\cite{pajeng} and analyzed using R with the tidyverse/ggplot2 stack to
compute communication load fractions and throughput metrics.

**** GPU Cluster

We conduct GPU experiments on the /poti/ cluster of the PCAD
platform. The cluster consists of five computational nodes, each
equipped with an Intel Core i7-14700KF processor at 3.4GHz (20 cores,
28 threads), 96GB of DDR5 RAM, an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070 GPU with
12GB of VRAM, and a 1Gbps Gigabit network interconnect. Experiments
use all five nodes with only RTX4070 accelerators, running the
optimized CUDA propagation kernel. The same tracing and analysis
toolchain used in the CPU experiments applies here, recording MPI
events at per-rank granularity to compute compute-load fractions
throughout the full execution window.

**** Experimental Design
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.experimental_design
:END:

The Design of Experiments is grounded in a reproducible,
multi-platform approach. All experiments are built using Nix
\cite{nix}, which guarantees a fully deterministic software
environment across every run and ensures that the compiled
application, its MPI library, and all analysis tools are pinned to
exact versions.

Beyond the standard metrics of compute load and execution time, we
also report throughput in MSamples/s, a common performance metric used
for the analysis of the reference Fletcher code base
\cite{serpa2018wscad, serpa2021ccpe}. This metric normalizes across
backends and problem sizes, enabling comparison of distributed
performance with a single-node baseline. Finally, we note that the
physical /poti/ cluster's GPU VRAM (12 GB) imposes a practical ceiling
on the maximum solvable domain per worker, a constraint that directly
influences the range of problem sizes evaluated in our GPU experiments
and motivates the use of simulation to explore configurations beyond
what the physical hardware can accommodate.

SimGrid SMPI \cite{degomme2017simulating} is employed for two
complementary reasons. First, the physical clusters have limited
interconnect bandwidth, preventing direct evaluation of high-speed
network configurations. Second, the GPU VRAM limitation described
below restricts the maximum problem sizes achievable on real
hardware. Together, these constraints motivate the use of simulation
to explore configurations beyond the physical platforms' capacity. To
this end, we define the SimGrid platform model with either 10 Gbps or
100 Gbps network bandwidths. A single /poti/ node runs the SMPI
simulations to ensure computation times match those on the real
platform. We adjust SMPI's emulation so that the compute kernel,
whether CPU- or GPU-based, executes without SMPI sampling, capturing
the full GPU or CPU behavior, including compute artifacts absent with
the sampling option. To ensure that simulated results faithfully
reproduce the behavior observed on the real /poti/ cluster, we follow
the calibration methodology described by Cornebize
\cite{Cornebize2022VariabilityMatters}, which provides a systematic
approach for extracting and modeling the characteristics of the MPI
runtime backend and the underlying network into accurate SimGrid
platform descriptions. This methodology involves profiling the MPI
implementation (OpenMPI in our case) to extract protocol thresholds,
such as the message size at which the communication library switches
from the eager to the rendezvous protocol, and measuring latency and
bandwidth curves for various message sizes. The SimGrid platform
description uses calibrated parameters to define network links with
realistic bandwidth-latency models, typically using a double-linear
model that captures the different slopes observed for small and large
messages. The calibration step ensures that both computation and
communication timings in simulation reproduce those observed on the
physical /poti/ platform within a bounded error margin of at most
15--17% for messages up to 1GiB with the largest halo size used in
our experiments. The combined effect of communication calibration and
faithful kernel emulation is that the compute-load distributions
closely mirror their real-cluster counterparts, enabling us to
extrapolate performance across the explored configurations with
confidence.

** Results
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.results
:END:

We present results from our implementation of the MPI-Based 3D
Anisotropic RTM with Fletcher's Method and Ghost Cell Halo Exchange,
first on real HPC clusters and then via SMPI-based simulation. We also
provide a comprehensive analysis of throughput in MSamples/s, a common
metric for the reference Fletcher code base \cite{serpa2021ccpe}.

The figures in this section are generated from PajeNG state files
\cite{pajeng} collected during each simulation or real execution.
Each state file records a per-rank timeline of MPI operations
(~MPI_Irecv~, ~MPI_Isend~, ~MPI_Waitall~) with start and end times.  The
visualization pipeline first synthesizes a ``Compute'' state by
inserting a synthetic task into each rank's timeline for the gaps
between consecutive MPI events, thereby reconstructing the full
compute-communication schedule. Note that this inserted state
comprises the whole CPU/GPU activities, including copying data back
and forth the GPU through the PCI bus at every iteration. For each
rank, the pipeline then produces two complementary views. The first is
a space-time Gantt chart (see Figure \ref{fig:real_cpu_gantt_256} for
an example) that plots each rank on the Y axis and time on the X axis,
drawing a colored rectangle for every MPI event and computed interval
at full temporal resolution. The second is an aggregated space-time
load chart (see Figure \ref{fig:real_cpu_load_256} for an example)
inspired by the StarVZ framework \cite{starvz}, which bins the full
execution into 100 uniform time intervals via time-slice integration:
for each rank-task pair, the pipeline computes what fraction of each
interval a task occupied, then stacks the Compute and ~MPI_Waitall~
fractions proportionally to produce a per-interval utilization profile
that abstracts away iteration-level detail and highlights the overall
balance between computation and communication throughout the run.

*** Throughput: Partitioning Topology as Critical Factor

Figure \ref{fig:msamples} presents the throughput in MSamples/s as a
function of the problem size for the single-node baseline, the real
five-node cluster execution, and the simulated scenarios with 5 and 8
workers at 10Gbps and 100Gbps. The most striking observation is the
dramatic gap between 5-worker and 8-worker configurations. At $512^3$,
the 8-worker simulations reach 8309 MSamples/s (10Gbps) and 9374
MSamples/s (100Gbps), while the corresponding 5-worker results plateau
at 3030 and 3215 MSamples/s, values that are below the single-node
baseline of 3870 MSamples/s. In other words, with 5 workers the
communication overhead is so severe that distributing the computation
yields \emph{negative} speedup.

#+begin_export latex
\begin{figure}[!htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{./img/msamples.pdf}
\caption{Throughput (MSamples/s) as a function of problem size for real and simulated executions, compared to the single-node baseline.}
\label{fig:msamples}
\end{figure}

The root cause lies in the partitioning shape imposed by
~MPI_Dims_create~. Since 5 is prime, it is forced into a $\{5, 1, 1\}$
slices decomposition, whereas 8 factors into a $\{2, 2, 2\}$ blocks
decomposition. As established in Section [[#sec.ghost_cells]] and Figure
\ref{fig:comm_cost}, the total communication boundary for slices grows
as $2(P - 1)L^2$, while for blocks it grows as $6(\sqrt[3]{P} -
1)L^2$. Concretely, for a $512^3$ domain with a stencil thickness of 4
cells:

- 5 workers $\{5,1,1\}$: each slab measures approximately
  $108 \times 512 \times 512$ and communicates with at most 2 neighbors, since
  the 1D partitioning produces no diagonal adjacencies. Each face halo
  is $4 \times 512 \times 512 = 1048576$ doubles (approximately 8~MB). With two
  fields ($P$ and $Q$) and up to two neighbors, the total
  communication per iteration reaches approximately 64MB.
- 8 workers $\{2,2,2\}$: each sub-cube measures $260\times260\times260$ (including ghost cells) and communicates with 7 neighbors: 3 on
  the same level and 4 on the adjacent level, including diagonal
  adjacencies. The face halos are $4 \times 256 \times 256 = 262144$ doubles
  (approximately 2MB) each, while edge and corner halos are
  substantially smaller ($4 \times 4 \times 256$ and $4 \times 4 \times 4$,
  respectively). The total communication per iteration amounts to
  approximately 25MB.

The block decomposition therefore reduces the per-worker communication
volume by approximately 2.5\times, from 64MB to 25MB, despite each worker
communicating with more neighbors (7 vs. 2). As expected from a
balanced partitioning, the computation time decreases proportionally
when moving from 5 to 8 workers: at $512^3$ on the 100Gbps simulated
network, total computation drops from approximately 28s to
approximately 14s, consistent with the $8/5 = 1.6$ factor in available
parallelism. This scaling of the computational component is the
expected behavior of a distributed implementation.

The MPI overhead, however, exhibits a disproportionately large
reduction that cannot be explained by the 2.5\times communication volume
difference alone. Furthermore, increasing the network bandwidth
tenfold (10Gbps to 100Gbps) barely improves the 5-worker throughput
(3030 to 3215 MSamples/s, +6%), indicating that the bottleneck in the
$\{5,1,1\}$ configuration is not the network bandwidth itself. The
precise cause of this behavior remains to be fully characterized
through detailed profiling; however, it is hypothesized that the large
face size of the $\{5,1,1\}$ slabs ($512 \times 512$ per face,
approximately 8MB per halo) triggers higher-latency MPI protocol paths
(such as the rendezvous protocol for large messages) and amplifies
fixed per-transfer costs (PCIe host--device copies, synchronization
handshakes) that are independent of network bandwidth and accumulate
over the 1001 iterations of the simulation.

** Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: sec.conclusion
:END:

This paper presented a distributed MPI implementation of the Fletcher
RTM application with 3D block decomposition and asynchronous ghost
cell communication, alongside GPU acceleration via CUDA. Experiments
on real HPC clusters exposed two sharply contrasting regimes. On
CPU-only machines, modest floating-point throughput naturally yields
near-perfect communication hiding across realistic network conditions:
the /cei/ cluster reaches approximately 87% compute load at $512^3$ on
its real 10Gbps network, and the compute fraction at $256^3$ already
achieves 75%, establishing that arithmetic intensity alone is
sufficient to mask communication on this class of hardware. On
GPU-equipped clusters, the picture inverts entirely: the raw
throughput of RTX4070 accelerators makes network bandwidth the
governing bottleneck, with /poti/ stalling near 20% compute load on a
1Gbps interconnect regardless of problem size. Calibrated SimGrid
SMPI simulations further demonstrate a smooth, bandwidth-driven
transition from communication-bound to computation-bound behavior:
increasing the interconnect from 1Gbps to 10Gbps raises GPU compute
load from \approx20% to \approx75--80%, and to \approx85--90% at 100Gbps, confirming
that the asynchronous ghost cell masking strategy is effective when
provided with adequate network capacity.

The boundary-to-volume analysis confirms the root cause: as problem
size grows, the stencil volume increases as $O(n^3)$ while the halo
surface grows only as $O(n^2)$, so a larger local domain should
improve masking. On GPU clusters, however, the accelerator processes
even the largest domain so rapidly that the Gigabit network remains the
overwhelming bottleneck. Increasing problem size alone is therefore
insufficient to achieve masking on this platform; the determining
factor is the ratio of network bandwidth to accelerator throughput.

The importance of proper domain segmentation is demonstrated by the
performance gap between prime and non-prime process counts. Since 5 is
prime, ~MPI_Dims_create~ forces a $\{5,1,1\}$ slices decomposition
with per-iteration communication volumes of approximately 64~MB,
whereas 8 processes factor into a $\{2,2,2\}$ block decomposition
requiring only approximately 25MB, a 2.5$\times$ reduction despite
communicating with more neighbors. As a result, the 5-worker
configuration falls below the single-node throughput baseline,
demonstrating negative speedup from distribution. This effect is so
severe that increasing the interconnect bandwidth tenfold (from 1Gbps
simulated to 10Gbps simulated) barely improves the 5-worker
throughput (+6%), indicating that the bottleneck in the
$\{5,1,1\}$ configuration extends beyond raw network bandwidth to
large-message protocol paths and fixed per-transfer costs. Prime
process counts are therefore as detrimental as slow networks, and
should be avoided when deploying block-decomposed stencil
applications.

Future work includes experiments on the Santos Dumont supercomputer to
evaluate the application on a production platform with a high-speed
interconnect, detailed simulation of the Santos Dumont network
topology to enable capacity-planning studies, integration of HIP
kernels for AMD GPU architectures, and a more thorough throughput
characterization across the full range of problem sizes and network
configurations that balance accelerator and network capacity. We also
intend to explore comparisons between CUDA- and HIP-aware
implementations of the MPI specification to evaluate how these compare
against typical non-RDMA MPI implementations from the perspective of
ghost cells.
